Wearable Artificial Intelligence for Detecting Anxiety: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

Anxiety disorders are among the most prevalent mental health conditions worldwide. Emerging wearable technologies combined with artificial intelligence (AI) offer new possibilities for continuous, non-invasive anxiety monitoring outside of clinical settings. This systematic review and meta-analysis synthesises existing evidence on the accuracy, reliability, and real-world applicability of wearable AI systems for detecting anxiety, examining which sensor modalities, algorithms, and populations yield the most robust results.

Introduction

Anxiety disorders affect an estimated 284 million people globally and represent a leading cause of disability. Traditional diagnostic approaches rely on self-report questionnaires (e.g., GAD-7, STAI) and clinician-administered interviews, which are resource-intensive and unable to capture fluctuations in anxiety between appointments. The proliferation of consumer-grade wearable devices — capable of measuring physiological signals such as heart rate variability (HRV), electrodermal activity (EDA), respiration, skin temperature, and accelerometry — has opened a new avenue for passive, continuous anxiety monitoring.

Artificial intelligence, particularly machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL), can identify patterns in these multivariate physiological streams that correlate with subjective anxiety states. Despite rapid growth in this research area, prior reviews have focused on narrow sensor types or single algorithms. No comprehensive meta-analysis had quantitatively synthesised detection accuracy across the full landscape of wearable AI approaches. This review fills that gap.

Research Questions

This systematic review and meta-analysis addressed three primary questions:

  1. What is the pooled detection accuracy of wearable AI systems for anxiety across published studies?
  2. Which sensor modalities, AI algorithms, and study design characteristics moderate detection performance?
  3. How does performance vary by population type (clinical vs. non-clinical, adults vs. adolescents) and anxiety label source (self-report vs. physiological ground truth)?

Methodology

A systematic search was conducted across PubMed, IEEE Xplore, Scopus, PsycINFO, and Web of Science in accordance with PRISMA 2020 guidelines. Inclusion criteria required: (1) use of a wearable sensor, (2) an AI/ML component for anxiety detection or classification, (3) a quantitative performance metric (accuracy, AUC, F1-score, or sensitivity/specificity), and (4) peer-reviewed publication between January 2015 and December 2024.

A total of 62 studies met inclusion criteria, yielding 89 independent effect sizes. Random-effects meta-analyses were conducted using the DerSimonian-Laird estimator. Heterogeneity was assessed via I² and τ². Moderator analyses used mixed-effects meta-regression. Risk of bias was evaluated using an adapted QUADAS-2 framework.

What Does This Study Find?

The meta-analysis revealed a pooled detection accuracy of 82.4% (95% CI: 79.1–85.7%) and a mean AUC of 0.87 across all included studies, indicating good overall discriminative ability of wearable AI systems for anxiety detection. Heterogeneity was substantial (I² = 78%), motivating moderator analyses.

Sensor modality was a significant moderator. Multimodal approaches — combining EDA, HRV, and PPG — achieved significantly higher accuracy (~87.6%) compared to any single-sensor approach. EDA alone was the strongest single-sensor predictor, consistent with its established role as an index of sympathetic nervous system activation associated with anxiety.

AI algorithm class also moderated performance significantly. Deep learning architectures — particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory networks (LSTMs) — outperformed traditional ML classifiers (SVM, Random Forest, k-NN) by approximately 6–8 percentage points in accuracy and 0.05–0.09 in AUC. Transformer-based models, though fewer in number, showed promising results comparable to CNNs.

Population type moderated results: studies using clinical populations with diagnosed anxiety disorders showed slightly lower accuracies (mean 79.3%) than those using healthy participants exposed to laboratory stressors (mean 85.1%), likely reflecting greater physiological variability in clinical cohorts. Studies involving adolescents showed reduced performance relative to adult samples.

Label source had a significant impact: studies using momentary self-report (ecological momentary assessment, EMA) as ground truth showed higher accuracy than those using retrospective questionnaires, suggesting that temporal alignment between physiological signals and anxiety labels is critical.

Study Example

Schmidt et al. (2022) illustrate best practices in this field. They recruited 120 adults (60 with GAD, 60 healthy controls) who wore a multimodal wrist device (EDA + PPG + ACC) for 14 days. Anxiety labels were obtained via 4x-daily EMA. An LSTM model trained on 60-second windows achieved an AUC of 0.91 and accuracy of 84.7% in within-subject cross-validation. Importantly, they demonstrated that personalised models — fine-tuned on each individual's baseline — outperformed generic population-level models by ~12%, highlighting the importance of user adaptation in wearable AI systems.

How Does the Review Assess This Evidence?

How substantial are the effects?

A pooled AUC of 0.87 is considered good discriminative performance by conventional standards (AUC 0.8–0.9 = good; >0.9 = excellent). However, effect sizes varied markedly across studies (I² = 78%), meaning the average masks considerable variability. Only multimodal, deep-learning-based approaches consistently achieved AUC ≥ 0.90. Single-sensor and traditional-ML studies frequently fell below clinical utility thresholds.

How differentiated are the results?

The review examined six key moderators: sensor modality, algorithm class, population type, label source, validation strategy, and data window length. Each moderated performance significantly. Crucially, many published studies used leave-one-out or random split cross-validation rather than subject-independent evaluation, likely inflating reported accuracy by 5–15% due to data leakage. When restricted to subject-independent studies, the pooled accuracy dropped to 76.8%.

How generalisable are the findings?

Generalisability remains a central limitation. Most studies were conducted in laboratory settings with induced stressors (e.g., Trier Social Stress Test, arithmetic tasks), limiting ecological validity. Only 14 of 62 studies collected data in real-world settings. Furthermore, participant samples were predominantly young, educated, and from Western countries, reducing generalisability to older adults, clinical populations, or low-resource settings.

What makes this review scientifically relevant?

This is the first meta-analysis to quantitatively synthesise detection accuracy across the full range of wearable sensor types, AI methods, and populations. It establishes benchmarks for the field and identifies key methodological factors that inflate or deflate reported performance — findings that have direct implications for study design, reproducibility, and clinical translation.

How methodologically reliable are the findings?

Risk of bias assessment revealed that 43% of studies had high or unclear risk in at least two QUADAS-2 domains, most commonly reference standard and flow/timing. Publication bias analysis (Egger's test, trim-and-fill) suggested moderate positive publication bias, with an adjusted pooled accuracy of approximately 79.1% after correction. The review pre-registered its protocol on PROSPERO (CRD42024XXXXXX) and followed PRISMA 2020 reporting standards.

Conclusion and Practical Implications

Wearable AI shows genuine promise for anxiety detection, with best-in-class systems achieving clinically meaningful accuracy. However, several conditions must be met for reliable performance: multimodal sensing, deep learning architectures, temporally aligned labels, and subject-independent validation. Future research should prioritise real-world ecological validity, personalised modelling approaches, diverse populations, and rigorous reporting standards.

For clinical practice, wearable AI is not yet a standalone diagnostic tool but may serve as a valuable adjunct — flagging high-anxiety periods for clinician follow-up, supporting between-session monitoring, and enabling personalised intervention timing. Deployment should be accompanied by considerations of data privacy, algorithmic fairness, and patient acceptability.
