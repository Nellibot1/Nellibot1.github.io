Anxiety disorders are among the most prevalent mental disorders worldwide. Wearable artificial intelligence (AI) — the combination of AI technology and wearable devices — has been widely used to detect and predict anxiety disorders automatically, objectively, and more efficiently. This systematic review and meta-analysis assesses the performance of wearable AI in detecting and predicting anxiety.

Introduction

Anxiety disorders (ADs) are one of the most common mental disorders worldwide. These include generalized AD, social AD, panic disorder, and various phobia-related disorders. It is estimated that 284 million people worldwide have been diagnosed with AD. Studies have reported that AD affects 14.5% to 33.7% of the population at least once in their lifetime.

Currently, ADs are diagnosed primarily through clinical observations, clinical histories, and self-report questionnaires (e.g., the State-Trait Anxiety Inventory). However, these approaches are hampered by significant limitations: subjectivity, reproducibility issues, shortage of mental health professionals, long clinical interview times, and extensive comorbidities. As a result, anxiety is commonly underdetected and undertreated despite its huge disease burden.

Wearable AI is the fusion of data obtained from wearables and sophisticated machine learning algorithms. If effectively used, wearable AI can greatly help in the accurate detection and prediction of anxiety. This review is the first systematic review and meta-analysis to evaluate the performance of wearable AI specifically for detecting and predicting anxiety.

Research Question

This systematic review and meta-analysis aimed to:

  • Assess the performance of wearable AI in detecting and predicting anxiety
  • Examine whether performance is moderated by algorithms, device type, data type, data source, reference standard, or validation method

Methodology

The review was conducted in accordance with PRISMA-DTA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Diagnostic Test Accuracy) and registered in PROSPERO (CRD42023387560).

The following 8 electronic databases were searched on October 3, 2022: MEDLINE (via Ovid), Embase (via Ovid), PsycINFO (via Ovid), CINAHL (via EBSCO), ACM Digital Library, Scopus, IEEE Xplore, and Google Scholar. Backward and forward reference list checking was also performed.

Two reviewers independently carried out study selection, data extraction, and risk-of-bias assessment. Risk of bias was assessed using a modified version of QUADAS-2 (Quality Assessment of Diagnostic Accuracy Studies–Revised). Evidence was synthesized using both a narrative (text and tables) and statistical (meta-analysis) approach.

Of the 918 records identified, 21 studies (2.3%) were included in this review.

What Does This Study Find?

A meta-analysis of results from 81% (17/21) of the studies revealed a pooled mean accuracy of 0.82 (95% CI: 0.71–0.89).

Meta-analyses of results from 48% (10/21) of the studies showed a pooled mean sensitivity of 0.79 (95% CI: 0.57–0.91) and a pooled mean specificity of 0.92 (95% CI: 0.68–0.98).

Moderator Analyses

Subgroup analyses demonstrated that the performance of wearable AI was not significantly moderated by any of the following factors:

  • Algorithms used
  • Aims of AI (detection vs. prediction)
  • Wearable devices used
  • Status of wearable devices (commercial vs. prototype)
  • Data types
  • Data sources
  • Reference standards
  • Validation methods

How Does the Review Assess This Evidence?

How Substantial Are the Effects?

A pooled accuracy of 0.82 is promising. However, the wide confidence interval (0.71–0.89) reflects considerable variability across studies. Sensitivity of 0.79 means approximately 1 in 5 anxious individuals would be missed. The performance is not yet sufficient for standalone clinical use.

How Credible Are the Results?

Risk of bias was assessed using a modified QUADAS-2 tool across all 21 included studies. The review followed PRISMA-DTA reporting standards and was pre-registered on PROSPERO, supporting transparency and credibility. The authors explicitly note the GIGO principle — the quality of the meta-analytic conclusions depends on the quality of the primary studies.

Conclusion and Practical Implications

Although wearable AI has the potential to detect anxiety, it is not yet advanced enough for clinical use. Until further evidence shows ideal performance, it should be used alongside other clinical assessments.

The authors specifically call for:

  • Wearable device companies to develop devices that can promptly detect anxiety and identify specific high-anxiety time points during the day
  • Further research to differentiate types of anxiety
  • Research to compare the performance of different wearable devices
  • Investigation of combining wearable data with neuroimaging data
